Meteorites:

Meteorites are pieces of rock or metal from space that survive their journey through Earth's atmosphere and land on the ground. They begin as small objects called meteoroids. When a meteoroid enters Earth's atmosphere, it burns up and creates a bright streak of light called a meteor, often known as a "shooting star." If part of it reaches the Earth's surface, it is called a meteorite.

Meteorites are formed from material left over from the creation of the Solar System about 4.6 billion years ago. They are mainly made of rock, iron, nickel, or a mixture of these materials. Most meteorites come from asteroids, although some have come from the Moon or Mars after large impacts blasted pieces of those worlds into space. Scientists study meteorites because they provide valuable clues about the early Solar System and how planets formed.

Some famous examples of meteorites include the Hoba Meteorite, the Chelyabinsk meteorite, and the Murchison Meteorite. The Hoba Meteorite, found in Namibia, is the largest meteorite ever discovered on Earth. The Chelyabinsk meteorite fell over Russia in 2013, creating a powerful shockwave that damaged buildings. The Murchison Meteorite, which landed in Australia in 1969, is well known because it contains organic molecules that help scientists study the origins of life.
